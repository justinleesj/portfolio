For this Electronics & Instrumentation (E&I) lab project, my teammates and I set out to recreate Mattel’s 1977 “Auto Race” handheld in discrete hardware. Rather than a full 3×7 LED matrix, we proved the core concept with a single 1 × 4 “lane.” Pressing a push‑button injects a “car” (a lit LED) at the top of the lane; the light then cascades down the row, giving the illusion of motion without any microcontroller or firmware. Although stripped down to one lane, the build still demonstrates pixel propagation and could be scaled to three lanes by replicating the same four‑LED chain.

The circuit relies on a 555 timer configured in astable mode to provide a steady clock, two 74HC73/74 JK flip‑flops to shift the logic “high” from LED 1 to LED 4, and a 74HC00 quad NAND used as an inverter when the button is released. With only a 555, four LEDs, four resistors, two capacitors, and the logic ICs, the parts list is refreshingly compact for a game replica. Component values were selected so that each LED stays on for ≈ 0.88 s and off for ≈ 0.19 s—long enough for the human eye to perceive motion while keeping the game brisk.

Bench measurements confirmed the design assumptions: current and voltage logs show each LED drawing ~3 mA in strict sequence, with only one diode active at a time. Using a DMM we also verified that the push‑button behaves as an open circuit once released, resetting the lane for the next “car”. A full TinkerCAD simulation prior to soldering caught several wiring errors and shortened physical build time, and swapping the timing capacitor to 100 µF slowed an initially over‑fast clock so the motion became visible. Overall, the project deepened my familiarity with timer circuits, flip‑flop state machines, and hardware‑first debugging—skills directly applicable to embedded electromechanical systems.
