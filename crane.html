As part of Johns Hopkins’ EN.530.216 Mechanics‑Based Design Laboratory, our three‑person “Da Vinci Design” team developed a benchtop crane capable of hoisting a 10‑lb (4.5 kg) load within a two‑minute window while staying under a US $25 material budget and a 0.5 in (12.7  mm) boom‑tip deflection limit. The project emphasized first‑principles sizing, safety factors, and rapid fabrication, giving us hands‑on experience in translating textbook beam and power‑transmission theory into a fully functional prototype.

The boom was modeled as a simply supported beam with a short overhang and finalized at a 0.5 in × 2 in acrylic cross‑section. Cross‑section dimensions were chosen by back‑calculating allowable deflection using Shigley’s elastic‑beam equations and then rounding up to accommodate a unified design factor of 1.5 that accounted for material data uncertainty, reliability, and simplified failure analysis. Two through‑bolts anchored the boom to a steel mounting plate, resisting bending moments while a filed cable guide and sanded edges minimized friction at the boom tip.

To convert the lab‑supplied gear‑motor’s 7.5 oz‑in stall torque into the lifting force required, we implemented a 4:1 spur‑gear reduction (1 in driver, 4 in driven, module 10) mounted between twin 4.5 in × 5.1 in side plates. Early tests revealed that friction—not torque—was the dominant loss mechanism, so we swapped the steel cable for a nylon line, lubricated all shaft interfaces, and routed the cable through two low‑friction pulleys (one on the load, one on the boom tip). These changes eliminated stalling and preserved the motor’s operating margin.

During evaluation the crane lifted the 10‑lb weight in 20.9 s—over five times faster than the specification—while peak boom deflection measured just 0.31 in, comfortably below the 0.5 in limit. Total material spend for the final build was US $24.44, well under the assigned cap, demonstrating that rigorous analysis can coexist with cost‑efficient design. Beyond meeting all technical metrics, the project refined our skills in acrylic machining, press‑fit/clearance‑fit tolerancing, and friction mitigation—competencies directly applicable to future electromechanical systems.
